911 Swatting, VoIP, and Doxxing

What is the definition of 911 swatting given in the article?
  • malicious act that involves making fraudulent 911 calls to cause emergency response teams, such as law enfrocement special weapons and tactic teams, or SWAT teams, to react forcefully to a nonexistent public threat.
The article gives 7 examples of 911 swatting. List 3 of them:
  1. celebrity swatting directed against public figures
  2. gamer swatting directed against adversaries in online gaming environments
  3. hate swatting and mean-spirited attacks motivated by bigotry, racism, homophobia, and personal animosity
What is the definition of ‘criminal doxing’ given in the article?
  • reveals personally identifiable information to embarrass, traumatize, intimidate, bully, harass, and encourage acts of violence against victims.
At the time of writing, how was 911 swatting typically prosecuted?
  • MY ANSWER: It is subsumed under other statutes dealing with fraud, civil rights, hate crimes, the national defense, and so on.
  • Fraud
In terms of ambiguous federal statutes, 911 swatting is similar to what other crime?
  • Domestic Terrorism
At what layer of the TCP/IP protocol does the ‘magic’ of VoIP take place according to the article?
  • Application Layer
The article discusses 3 differences between VoIP and most other packet-based applications within the TCP/IP protocol suite. List 2 of them:
  1. VoIP uses IPv4 and IPv6 packet payloads as the carriers of the audio/video media encodings
  2. hardware/software connection between a computer or computer system and some media appliances that are compatible w/ telephony
The author mentions some major deficiencies in the Truth in Caller ID Act of 2009. Name one.
  • It focuses on the intent of the source rather than the activity.
What were the two rules introduced by the FCC to enable law enforcement agencies to identify the source of 911 calls that took effect in 2020?
  • Kari’s Law and RAY BAUM’s
What federal statutes relate to 911 swatting and doxxing?
  • There are no federal statute that relates to 911 swatting and doxxing

Equifax and the Latest Round of Identity Theft Roulette

What data was leaked in the Equifax breach?
  • Personal data on 145 million people
What was the nature of the vulnerability in the Equifax hack?
  • Vulnerability in Apache Struts server software.
What was the specific attack vector?
  • Struts-parser had incorrect exception handling during file uploads that created an attack vector through execute commands via #cmd in content-type HTTP headers

TLDR; execute commands

According to Forbes Magazine, what penalties did the CEO of Equifax receive from the Board of Directors?
  • was forced to retire and received 90 million dollars
To what extent was information about the Struts vulnerability known before the attack?
  • The company was notified about the needed patch and the following day was instructed to apply the patch within 48 hours.
Was Equifax aware that a patch was available for the Struts vulnerability? If so, how much time did they delay in applying the patch?
  • The vulnerability was known three months before they applied the patch when the attack occurred.
Describe the ‘too big to fail era’?
  • a large company’s failure would hurt their economy
Are credit reporting companies held liable for PII data loss?
  • No due to lack of right to privacy

Codes of Ethics in a Post-Truth World

According to the author, what do codes of ethics do?
  • To systematize ideals that purport to clarify avowed core principles of organizations, corporations, governments, groups, and so on.

TLDR; clarify core principles of organizations, corps, govs, groups, etc.

List 3 circumstances that might trigger the enforcement of codes of ethics.
  1. litigation
  2. personnel issues
  3. management crises
According to the author, which of the following might have caused the MSU gymnastics scandal: code of ethics neglect or ignorance of the rules.
  • neither. the scandal was not a product of code neglect or ignorance of the rules.

TLDR; neither

What is the most likely cause of recurrent professional ethics violations?
  • culture of tolerance for improper behavior
Are codes of ethics to be understood as prescriptive?
  • No. It is a mistake to associate codes of conduct with prescriptive behavior.
List two likely consequences of violations of professional codes of ethics
  1. fines
  2. incarceration

OTHER: professional repimand and license revoked

What was the leadership of the American Psychological Association asked to do by the Bush administration?
  • modifed the APA code of ethics to weaken the prohibition against doing harm to patients and subjects by inserting a few caveats. TLDR; basically to corrupt APA’s code to endorse their own special branch of torture

OTHER: change their code of ethics

Why was the Bush administration looking for ethical cover for their enhanced interrogation program?
  • ethical cover was demanded by the DOD and CIA–the agencies who would have implemented this program and conduct the ‘enhanced interrogation’

TLDR; demanded by the DOD and CIA

Give one example of the corruption of a code of professional ethics
  • The APA case (American Psychology Association)
According to the author, does the fact that moral codes are frequently ignored negate their overall importance?
  • No

TLDR; does not negate their overall importance

What does the ‘Milgram experiment’ purport to show?
  • the enormous effect of authority figures on some people when it comes to the circumvention of normative ethical ideals.

The Thousand Talents Program Prosecutions in Context
